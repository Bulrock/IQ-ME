# Flynn effects — cohort drift in cognitive scores

The Flynn effect names a steady rise in measured IQ scores across the twentieth century. James Flynn documented the pattern across many countries and many test instruments. The size of the rise is roughly three points per decade.

The cause of the Flynn effect is debated. Better nutrition, better schooling, more exposure to abstract reasoning tasks, smaller family sizes, and the spread of testing itself have all been proposed. The point relevant to IQ-ME is not the cause but the consequence: a percentile anchor calibrated against one cohort overstates ability when applied to a later cohort.

The Flynn effect has slowed in some countries since the late twentieth century. Other countries continue to show steady rises. The pattern is not uniform.

IQ-ME's norming sample, SAPA, collects responses on a rolling basis. The calibration data therefore reflects a recent cohort. A test-taker comparing their result to a percentile from a cognitive test calibrated in the 1980s would see a different anchor for the same underlying ability. The two anchors are not contradictory; they are two different reference populations.

A test-taker should treat the IQ-scale anchor on the result page as a recent-cohort anchor. A score that would have been at the 90th percentile in the 1980s norming data is likely at a lower percentile in SAPA. The screener does not adjust for Flynn effects; it documents them.

The retest-effects page and the cultural-variance page address other sources of drift between test and population. Together, the three pages describe the most load-bearing limits on what a percentile anchor in the result page does and does not mean.

Every instrument has a validity envelope. The envelope names the population, conditions, and use cases under which the instrument's measurements can be trusted. Outside the envelope, the same instrument produces numbers that look like measurements but are not.

IQ-ME's envelope is narrow on purpose. The instrument is valid as a screener for fluid reasoning in adults who can complete a 16-item visual session unimpaired by acute distress, sleep deprivation, or substances. The envelope reflects the population the item set was calibrated against and the conditions under which the calibration data was collected.

The envelope has three zones. The first is the valid zone: adults in non-distress conditions complete the session as the instrument was designed for. The second is the partial-validity zone: adults with mild fatigue, transient distraction, or other moderate impairments produce noisier estimates but still interpretable ones. The third is the not-valid zone: clinical diagnosis, time-pressured assessment, employment screening, and stand-in multi-domain IQ batteries are all outside the envelope. A score from outside-envelope use is a number, not a measurement.

The validity-envelope diagram (rendered as a designed visual asset on this page and on the consent scene) shows the three zones explicitly. The diagram is the teachable form of the envelope; this prose is the supporting form.

A test-taker who suspects they are outside the envelope on any given day should treat the resulting score with that suspicion. The screener does not enforce wakefulness, sobriety, or attention; the validity envelope is a self-honest contract between the test-taker and the methodology.

The instrument's design choices reflect the envelope. There is no time pressure, because performance under time pressure is outside what the calibration data supports. There is no provider-side history, because a screener is not a longitudinal-monitoring instrument. The result page caveats the score with the envelope's most load-bearing boundaries.

Non-clinical posture — what IQ-ME is and is not

IQ-ME is a non-clinical screener. It is not a diagnostic. It is not a credential. It is not an educational-placement tool. It is not a basis for employment or legal decisions. These boundaries are not legal fine print; they are how the project chooses to behave.

The screener is published as a public methodology corpus alongside the assessment. A reader who never takes the test can read the methodology and form an opinion of what the instrument does. The methodology corpus carries equal weight with the result page. That equality is by design: a result without its methodology is a number without its meaning.

The instrument is not a substitute for a clinical assessment. A reader with concerns about cognitive functioning, learning, attention, or any other dimension of mental life should speak with a qualified clinician. A clinician brings observation across time, multi-domain testing, history-taking, and judgment that no 16-item screener can substitute for. The screener is a starting point for one specific question — what is a calibrated estimate of fluid reasoning under self-paced conditions — and nothing more.

The instrument is not a credential. There is no badge, no certificate, no shareable score artefact, no public leaderboard. The result page is intentionally un-screenshot-friendly. The project's design choices follow from the position that a screener of one narrow ability should not be a status surface. People who would use a score as a status object are not the population the screener exists to help.

The instrument is not an educational-placement tool. A score should not inform school admission, scholarship eligibility, gifted-and-talented sorting, or any other gatekeeping decision. The norming sample, the single-session structure, and the narrow construct all preclude that use. Educational placement decisions require multi-domain assessment over time by qualified professionals.

The instrument is not a basis for employment or legal decisions. No employer should use a score to screen candidates. No court should rely on a score as evidence of capacity. The project's distribution model — no telemetry, no server-side storage, no provider-side history — makes such use technically harder, but the moral position stands on its own: a fluid-reasoning screener is not a measure of fitness for work or legal capacity.

The instrument's posture is published. The methodology corpus, the source code, the item set, the scoring engine, and the design rationale are all open. A reader can verify every claim against the implementation. The project commits to that transparency as a check on the project itself: the moment the implementation diverges from the methodology, both surfaces fail in CI before any result is shown.

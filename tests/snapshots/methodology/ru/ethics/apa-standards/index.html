The American Psychological Association, the American Educational Research Association, and the National Council on Measurement in Education jointly publish the Standards for Educational and Psychological Testing. The current edition is the 2014 revision. These standards are the field's reference document for what a defensible cognitive instrument looks like.

IQ-ME is not a clinical or regulatory instrument. The screener is not submitted to any standards body. The standards are advisory for IQ-ME, not regulatory. The screener still aligns with several of the standards' load-bearing provisions on its own initiative.

The first relevant provision is score transparency. The standards call for clear public documentation of what an instrument measures, how it produces scores, and what the scores mean. IQ-ME ships a 30-page methodology corpus that documents all three. The result page links into the corpus from every score surface.

The second relevant provision is norming disclosure. The standards call for explicit description of the norming sample, including its known biases and limitations. IQ-ME's norming pages document the SAPA sample, the representativeness skew, and the Flynn effect openly.

The third relevant provision is validity-envelope disclosure. The standards call for explicit statements of what an instrument can and cannot measure, and for what populations and uses it is and is not appropriate. IQ-ME's validity-envelope page and limitations section make this explicit. The result page caveats the score with the most load-bearing of these boundaries.

The standards are not a checklist a screener passes or fails. They are a framework. IQ-ME's choice to align with the framework reflects the project's broader posture: be honest about what the instrument is, document the limits openly, do not pretend to authority the instrument does not have.

The SAPA sample is large. It is also skewed. The instrument's percentile anchor inherits both properties.

SAPA respondents skew young. The median age is well under the general-population median. A test-taker over 50 is taking a test calibrated against a younger group, and the percentile anchor reflects that.

SAPA respondents skew online. The platform reaches people who visit cognitive-research sites voluntarily. A test-taker reached through a clinical referral, an employer, or a school is taking a test calibrated against a population that selected itself in.

SAPA respondents skew English-speaking. The site is in English. The Russian-language and Polish-language audiences IQ-ME explicitly serves are not represented in the calibration data. Epic 7 will ship per-language tail-scene content reviewed by clinical-register reviewers, but the underlying norming sample remains SAPA.

SAPA respondents skew educated. Online cognitive research attracts people with higher educational attainment than the general population. The IQ-scale anchor is therefore biased upward relative to a general-population norm: a percentile of 50 in SAPA is approximately a percentile higher than 50 in a general population.

The screener documents this skew honestly. The result page caveats the score. The cultural-variance page makes the cross-cultural piece explicit. The Flynn-effects page makes the cohort piece explicit. A test-taker who reads the result page caveat and follows the links arrives at this page, which is the canonical surface for the skew.

A test-taker should treat the percentile anchor as approximate and the IQ-scale anchor as approximate. The uncertainty band is the honest signal. The skew is a known limit of the screener, not a defect.

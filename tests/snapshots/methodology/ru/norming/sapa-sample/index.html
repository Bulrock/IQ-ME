The percentile-to-IQ-scale transform in IQ-ME was calibrated against the SAPA Project. SAPA stands for the Synthetic Aperture Personality Assessment. It is an online cognitive-research platform run by an academic group.

SAPA collects cognitive-test responses from volunteers who visit the site. The sample is large — hundreds of thousands of respondents — and the data are open. Researchers across the world have used the SAPA data to calibrate cognitive instruments.

IQ-ME draws its norming data from the SAPA ICAR-MR responses. The calibration produces the parameters a and b for each item in the pool. It also produces the population mean and standard deviation that anchor the IQ-scale transform.

The choice of SAPA reflects three trade-offs. The first is data availability. SAPA is one of the few open cognitive-data sources large enough to support stable item calibration. The second is item match. SAPA used ICAR-MR items, which is what IQ-ME uses. The third is honesty. SAPA's population is known and documentable; commercial cognitive-test norms are typically not.

The sample has a known skew. The representativeness page describes the skew in detail. The Flynn-effects page describes the cohort effect that affects SAPA and every other modern norming sample. A test-taker should read both pages to understand what the percentile anchor in their result page does and does not represent.
